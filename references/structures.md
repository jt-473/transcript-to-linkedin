# Post structures

Each structure below has a shape, a template, and what usually goes wrong when it's written by an AI instead of a person. Use the shape as a skeleton, not a fill-in-the-blank form — real posts break these rules constantly, and a post that too visibly follows the template is itself an AI tell.

**A note on formatting, based on real high-performing posts:** the common thread across strong LinkedIn posts isn't dense, essay-style paragraphs — it's short, single-thought lines with real whitespace between them, closer to how someone would pause between thoughts out loud than how they'd write a report. A few takeaways are often numbered plainly (`1/`, `2/`, `3/`) rather than buried in prose. None of this is the same as the AI-tell version (bolded inline headers, emoji standing in for bullets, a "Conclusion" section) — it's closer to spoken rhythm than to formatting for its own sake. Apply this rhythm across all five structures below, not just the ones that look listy.

## 1. Storytelling

**When to use:** the transcript has one specific scene — a moment, a conversation, a day, a decision — with a beginning, a turn, and an end.

**Shape:**
- Hook: drop the reader into the moment, not into a setup. Start mid-scene or mid-thought, not "Today I want to share a story about..."
- Anecdote: the concrete details — what happened, said in the order it happened, with the small specific stuff left in (what they were wearing, what time it was, what the other person actually said).
- The turn: the moment their understanding changed. This is the actual point of the post — don't rush it.
- Takeaway: what they'd tell someone else now, in their own words, not a mission-statement version of it.
- Soft CTA (optional): a real question to the reader, or nothing at all.

**Avoid:** starting with "Let me tell you a story," resolving the turn in one flat sentence right after building it up for three paragraphs, ending on "the moral of the story is."

**Example shape (not to be copied verbatim, just the rhythm):**
```
I got rejected from 11 internships before this one said yes.

Number 7 was the one that actually broke something in me. Recruiter called
to reject me and said, off-script, "you interview like you're apologizing
for being here." I didn't have a comeback. I just said thanks and hung up.

I thought about that line for weeks. Started noticing I really was doing
it — hedging every answer, undercutting anything good I said with a "but
I'm still learning."

Interview 12 I stopped doing that. Not more confident, just... stopped
apologizing mid-sentence. Got the offer a week later.

Still not sure I earned it more than the other 11. Just stopped talking
myself out of it in real time.
```

## 2. Listicle / tactical

**When to use:** the transcript is mostly advice, process, or "here's what worked" — several distinct, separable takeaways rather than one scene.

**Shape:**
- Hook: name the result or the situation plainly, no throat-clearing.
- Brief context: one or two lines, just enough to make the list make sense.
- The list: short, punchy, each item earns its place — cut anything that's filler or too obvious to say. Number or use line breaks; don't force parallel grammatical structure so hard it stops sounding like a person.
- CTA (optional): invite people to add their own, or nothing.

**Avoid:** exactly 5 items every time (vary the count — 3, 4, 6, whatever the content actually supports), bolding every list item's first few words like a headline, items that are all restatements of the same idea in different words, ending with "which one resonates with you?"

**Example shape:**
```
Did 6 case interviews this month before I actually knew what I was doing.
Here's what actually moved the needle, in order:

1. Stopped trying to sound smart in the first 30 seconds. Just repeated
the prompt back and asked one clarifying question.
2. Wrote the framework on paper before saying anything out loud. Sounds
obvious, changed everything.
3. Practiced with people who'd actually give me hard feedback, not just
friends being nice.
4. Timed myself. I was spending 8 minutes on math that should take 2.

Still get nervous. Just waste less time on the wrong things now.
```

## 3. Contrarian / hot take

**When to use:** the transcript has the person pushing back on common advice, expressing a strong or slightly unpopular opinion, or reacting against something they were told.

**Shape:**
- Hook: state the take plainly and early — don't bury it under "unpopular opinion but" as a crutch (it's fine if it fits their actual voice, but the take itself should be able to stand without the label).
- Reasoning: why they believe it, grounded in what actually happened to them, not abstract argument.
- Nuance: where the common advice does still apply, or who it's actually for — this is what keeps it from reading like empty edge-lording.
- CTA (optional): invite disagreement genuinely, don't fish for validation.

**Avoid:** hot takes that aren't actually contrarian ("networking matters" is not a hot take), skipping the nuance section entirely (makes it read as arrogant rather than earned), stacking three separate contrarian claims into one post.

**Example shape:**
```
"Apply to 100 jobs" is bad advice for most students.

I did that first. Got maybe 4 responses, none of them jobs I actually
wanted, and burned two months customizing resumes for roles I'd have
turned down anyway.

What actually worked: picked 12 companies I cared about, found one person
at each on LinkedIn, and just asked them real questions about the job.
Got 3 offers from that list.

Not saying volume never works. If you genuinely don't care which job you
get, it probably does. I just didn't, and pretending I did wasted my time.
```

## 4. Before/after (lesson from a mistake)

**When to use:** the transcript centers on something that went wrong, and what changed afterward.

**Shape:**
- Situation: quick, concrete setup of what they were doing and what they believed at the time.
- The mistake/struggle: what actually happened, said plainly — no softening it into something more flattering than it was.
- What changed: the specific thing they did differently, not a vague "I learned to be more careful."
- Lesson: stated in their own terms, tied back to the specific mistake, not generalized into something that could apply to any mistake ever.

**Avoid:** mistakes that are humble-brags in disguise ("my biggest flaw is I care too much"), lessons so generic they'd fit under any post ("always believe in yourself"), skipping the actual mistake to get to the redemption faster.

**Example shape:**
```
Showed up to my first big presentation with slides I built the night
before. Didn't rehearse once. Told myself I "work better under pressure."

I didn't. Lost my place twice, and my manager had to jump in to answer a
question I should've had covered.

Next presentation, I ran through it out loud, alone in my room, four
times. Felt stupid doing it. Went fine.

Turns out "I work better under pressure" was just a story I told myself
so I didn't have to practice.
```

## 5. Milestone/gratitude announcement

**When to use:** the material centers on a real milestone — a new job, an offer, a graduation, an acceptance — especially when there are specific people to credit.

This is the structure most likely to collapse into AI slop, because the generic version of it ("I'm thrilled to announce... incredibly grateful for this opportunity... here's to new beginnings!") is everywhere and technically still gets engagement on LinkedIn regardless of quality, since people reflexively congratulate milestone posts. That's not a reason to write the generic version — it's a reason to make sure the specific version, which is just as easy to write from real material, is the one that goes out.

**Shape:**
- The announcement, stated plainly in one line — no "I'm excited to share" windup before it.
- One specific, concrete reason this one mattered — not "incredibly grateful for this opportunity," but the actual thing that made it hard or meaningful (see Step 3 in the main skill for how to pull this from the material).
- Real people credited by name, for a specific reason each, not a tagged list with one generic "for believing in me" applied to all of them. If real names aren't available, say so and use a placeholder the user is expected to fill in — never invent a name to make the thank-you list look fuller.
- Optional short line on what's next, only if there's something concrete to say — not "looking forward to growing professionally."

**Avoid:** "I'm thrilled/excited/humbled to announce," "incredibly grateful for this opportunity," a wall of tagged names with the same generic reason attached to each, "here's to new beginnings," a hashtag pile at the end that just restates the announcement (`#NewJob #CareerGrowth #Excited`).

**Example shape:**
```
Starting as a data analyst at [Company] next month.

Applied to this one twice. Got rejected the first time for not having
enough SQL experience, so I spent three months doing it badly on my own
projects until it stopped being bad.

[Name] reviewed my resume four separate times before I felt okay sending
it. [Name] did a mock interview with me at 11pm the night before, unpaid,
because I asked.

Still don't feel like I "made it." Just feels like the SQL finally worked.
```
